I swore to myself: this one's not gonna be another half-assed attempt.

Looking at the quality of the code Claude produces, I was floored. It's genuinely incredible to type out the architecture of a project and the business logic and watch it come to life. Come to life? But for a project to actually come to life, it has to be more than a CRUD running on localhost:3000.

I started by building this blog. Ten minutes and it was done. But that's normal. Before Claude Code, I could've built a blog like this in a few hours. I gained productivity. But the problem with my personal blog was never the code, it was always the fact that I don't write, I don't post, and I keep endlessly refining a system I never actually use.

I came face to face with a brutal truth: the code bottleneck was wiped off the map, but code was never my bottleneck. I already knew how to program before AI. I picked up complex concepts easily and always figured out all kinds of projects through research. The problem is that when I'm building something for myself, I don't bring the same sense of responsibility I bring when someone's paying me to build it. Which contradicts a philosophy I've got deeply wired into me: working for yourself is always gonna beat working for someone else.

The truth is that building a product is a fucking chore. And it was never about knowing how to code or not. If the real problem were code, we'd have way more rich software developers walking around. The folks building successful SaaS today already had what mattered before AI showed up: clarity on which problem to solve and the willingness to actually ship. AI just amplified that.

I realized I've always built tons of projects, always with a lot of love, but with a useless perfectionism that just made me lose faith in myself. And that turned into a cycle: build MVP, refine endlessly with zero users, get another idea, switch projects. With the new AI tools, this cycle just got faster. In one week of vibe coding I already built 4 projects. The problem was never the code itself, and it's hard to admit this, but I sank all my projects because I never had the balls to ship them.

Even when it came to selling services, I was always the problem. Thinking about it now, I didn't make it in the field not because I couldn't code, but because I lacked commercial maturity when it came to defining scope and aligning expectations. And that kind of problem, no AI in the world is gonna fix.

But I'm actually decided this time. AI reignited something in me that hadn't burned in a long time. **I want to be a successful programmer.** I'm gonna make money with technology and make people's lives better with my skills, but for that, I need to shift my mindset to identify and solve real problems, the way I used to at the companies I worked for.

In my last post I said that [artificial intelligence can't beat your natural stupidity](/posts/burrice-natural). And the irony was that, in my first week using AI to code, I realized my problem was always my natural stupidity when it comes to business. But knowing that is half the battle. The other half is acting differently, and that's why this post exists. Now that I'm not spending so much time coding, I can grow as an entrepreneur and as a professional.

Communication matters, but action is what gets you out of where you are. I still don't know exactly what I want from my career, but I'll keep writing here and updating you on my progress. I like writing, I like talking to you, and thanks for reading this far.
