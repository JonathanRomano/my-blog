I feel a bit dumb doing this for the "first" time in 2026, considering I've wanted to start a personal blog for at least 8 years.

## Why does this blog exist?

I'm going to document here my learning process in this new era of programming, where artificial intelligence does the bitch work and we mere mortals reserve ourselves to the task of just having **common sense**.

I worked for a few good years as an independent software developer, but I lost the spark for the field as time went on.

## My career as a developer

It was a huge frustration. I liked programming, but I never made money the way I expected to. I never managed to land a job at a big company, and I ended up focusing on freelance gigs.

The problem with being an independent developer and working as a freelancer is that you depend a lot on your communication skills. And even when I tried hard, I couldn't communicate that well. I got stiffed a few times and realized that most people looking for a programmer don't know what they want — and don't even know what a programmer is capable of doing.

What did I do in the face of all that? I went abroad to try the immigrant life. I actually worked as a developer for a year in Portugal, but I came to Germany when I got the chance.

## Ironically

Here in this cold country, famous for its "closed-off people" and "barely any social life", is where I developed my communication skills the most. I'm currently working at a bar, and when you have to deal with people every day in a completely different language, communication stops being an important skill and becomes a matter of survival.

## Why I decided to come back

Even with no intention of going back to the field, I always had the habit of telling everyone I'm a "programmer by profession". One of those times I said I was a programmer, a friend asked me for help using Claude Code. He showed me what he was doing, told me a bit about what he was expecting, and why he was frustrated with the results Claude Code was delivering — and right there, in that moment, I realized Claude was in the same position I'd been in so many times. Someone was making a completely vague request, and the poor thing was busting its ass to deliver what was asked, but the prompts he was using made me understand one very important thing: **artificial intelligence cannot overcome your natural stupidity.**

Since I've worked in the field and felt firsthand what it's like to fix a bug in production with the entire team breathing down your neck because they need the system to work, I know I'm more qualified than a complete beginner. Could this be a **window of opportunity?**

I'm not sure.

Like a good code whore, I sold myself back to the tech industry. But now with the idea of being an independent programmer powered by artificial intelligence. 🚀
